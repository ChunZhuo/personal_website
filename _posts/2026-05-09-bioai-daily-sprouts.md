Search date: 2026-05-09. Window: roughly the last 30 days, with a few high-relevance early-April papers included. Sources prioritized: Nature portfolio, Bioinformatics, Nucleic Acids Research, and PLOS Computational Biology.

## Papers

1. **Digital twins of ex vivo human lungs enable accurate and personalized evaluation of therapeutic efficacy**  
Nature Biotechnology, 2026-05-04. [DOI/link](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41587-026-03121-4)  
Summary: Builds personalized ex vivo lung digital twins for evaluating therapeutic response in a controlled human-organ setting.  
Why it matters: Digital-twin models are moving toward experimentally grounded, patient-specific biology rather than only retrospective simulation.  
Tags: digital twins; translational biology; precision medicine; computational biology

2. **DNA-guided CRISPR-Cas12a effectors for programmable RNA recognition and cleavage**  
Nature Biotechnology, 2026-05-01. [DOI/link](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41587-026-03120-5)  
Summary: Describes DNA-guided Cas12a effectors that can recognize and cleave RNA programmatically.  
Why it matters: Expands the programmable nucleic-acid toolbox for RNA targeting, diagnostics, and synthetic-biology workflows.  
Tags: CRISPR; RNA; synthetic biology; biotechnology

3. **Learning Protein Representations with Conformational Dynamics**  
Bioinformatics, 2026-05-05. [DOI/link](https://academic.oup.com/bioinformatics/advance-article/doi/10.1093/bioinformatics/btag254/8669790)  
Summary: Introduces DynamicsPLM, a protein language model approach that conditions representations on computational ensembles of protein conformations.  
Why it matters: Protein models often compress a dynamic molecule into one structure; incorporating conformational variability should help function, interaction, and activity prediction.  
Tags: protein language models; protein dynamics; AI4Bio; structural bioinformatics

4. **Synthetic data enables human-grade microtubule analysis with foundation models for segmentation**  
PLOS Computational Biology, 2026-05-05. [DOI/link](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1013901)  
Summary: Uses synthetic data and foundation-model segmentation to automate microtubule image analysis at near-human quality.  
Why it matters: Synthetic training data can reduce annotation bottlenecks for cell-biology imaging, especially when structures are hard to segment consistently.  
Tags: bioimage informatics; foundation models; synthetic data; cell biology

5. **Discovering proteo-transcriptomic networks via biologically informed heterogeneous graph learning**  
Nucleic Acids Research, 2026-04-30. [DOI/link](https://academic.oup.com/nar/article/54/8/gkag386/8665348)  
Summary: Applies biologically informed heterogeneous graph learning to infer links between proteomic and transcriptomic signals.  
Why it matters: Graph learning is becoming a practical bridge between molecular layers, helping expose regulatory structure that single-omics views miss.  
Tags: graph learning; proteomics; transcriptomics; systems biology

6. **Probabilistic modelling of single-cell bisulfite sequencing data with MethylVI**  
Nature Machine Intelligence, 2026-04-28. [DOI/link](https://www.nature.com/articles/s42256-026-01225-9)  
Summary: Presents MethylVI, a deep probabilistic model for single-cell bisulfite sequencing that separates biological methylation signal from technical nuisance variation.  
Why it matters: Single-cell methylome data are sparse and protocol-sensitive; this gives the field a reusable scvi-tools-style model for integration, atlas mapping, and differential methylation.  
Tags: single-cell; DNA methylation; probabilistic modeling; AI4Bio

7. **Systematically decoding pathological morphologies and molecular profiles with unified multimodal embedding**  
Nature Methods, 2026-04-24. [DOI/link](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41592-026-03070-5)  
Summary: Introduces Multi-Embed, an interpretable multimodal framework linking pathology morphology with multilayer molecular profiles.  
Why it matters: Multimodal pathology is shifting from image-only prediction toward integrated morphology-to-molecular reasoning.  
Tags: computational pathology; multimodal learning; molecular profiling; machine learning

8. **Direct RNA sequencing and signal alignment reveal RNA structure ensembles in a eukaryotic cell**  
Nature Methods, 2026-04-24. [DOI/link](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41592-026-03069-y)  
Summary: Combines direct RNA sequencing and signal alignment to study RNA structure ensembles inside eukaryotic cells.  
Why it matters: Direct sequencing signal can expose RNA structural heterogeneity in cellular context, supporting richer models of RNA regulation.  
Tags: RNA structure; direct RNA sequencing; computational biology; transcriptomics

9. **Unveiling gene perturbation effects through gene regulatory networks inference from single-cell transcriptomic data**  
PLOS Computational Biology, 2026-04-15. [DOI/link](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1014067)  
Summary: Builds predictive gene-regulatory-network models from single-cell transcriptomic data to simulate perturbation effects across biological settings.  
Why it matters: Perturbation prediction is a core step toward model-guided experimental design in single-cell biology.  
Tags: gene regulatory networks; single-cell; perturbation prediction; machine learning

10. **Artificial allosteric protein switches with machine-learning-designed receptors**  
Nature Biotechnology, 2026-04-15. [DOI/link](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41587-026-03081-9)  
Summary: Shows that machine-learning-designed ligand-binding domains can act as receptors in artificial allosteric protein switches and biosensors.  
Why it matters: Connects protein design to functional synthetic-biology devices, including logic gates, engineered cells, and bioelectronic hormone sensing.  
Tags: protein design; synthetic biology; biosensors; AI4Bio

## Watch list

- Single-cell foundation infrastructure is broadening beyond RNA into methylation, spatial and multimodal molecular measurements.
- Protein language modeling is moving from static sequences or structures toward dynamics-aware and uncertainty-aware representations.
- Computational pathology and bioimage analysis are becoming more multimodal, with stronger links between images and molecular profiles.
- AI4Bio papers with experimental validation remain more compelling than purely benchmark-driven model papers.
